<h1>Articles</h1>

<p1>
Erdic includes four articles that are used before nouns, <i>je</i>, <i>jee</i>, <i>ij/yij</i>, and <i>ijen/yijen</i>. They are differentiated via plurality and definitiveness, as shown in the table below:
<br>
<br>
</p1>

<table>
  <tr>
    <th></th>
    <th>Singular</th>
    <th>Plural</th>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <th>Definite</th>
    <td>Je</td>
    <td>Jee</td>
  </tr>
  <tr>
    <th>Indefinite</th>
    <td>Ij</td>
    <td>*Ijen</td>
  </tr>
</table>

<p2>
<i>*Ijen is rarely used in modern Erdic</i>
</p2>

<p5>
<br>
<br>
<b>Je</b> glas m'vitur.
<br>
<i>Translated: <b>The</b> woman sees me.</i>
</p5>

<h2><i>Je</i> Liaison</h2>

<p6>
When preceeding a word that begins in a vowel sound, <i>je</i> becomes <i>j'</i>.
</p6>

<p7>
<br>
<br>
<b>J'</b>om m'vitur.
<br>
<i>Translated: <b>The</b> man sees me.</i>
</p7>

<h2><i>Yij</i> and <i>Yijen</i></h2>

<p8>
When proceeding a word that terminates in a vowel sound, <i>ij</i> becomes <i>yij</i> and <i>ijen</i> becomes <i>yijen</i>
<br>
<br>
Sie <b>yij</b> glas.
<br>
<i>Translated: You are <b>a</b> woman.</i>
</p8>

<h2>Decline in <i>Ijen</i> Usage</h2>

<p9>
In modern Erdic, it is rare to see <i>ijen</i> used. It is usually dropped alltogether, however it still remains in some idiomatic expressions.
</p9>
